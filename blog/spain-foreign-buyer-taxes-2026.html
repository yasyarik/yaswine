Foreign Buyer Taxes in Spain 2026: Full Cost Breakdown

Foreign buyers in Spain can expect a comprehensive tax structure in 2026, encompassing purchase taxes, annual holding costs, and capital gains upon sale. Understanding these costs is crucial for accurate budgeting and successful real estate investment in the Spanish market.

What Are the Primary Taxes for Foreign Property Buyers in Spain?

The primary taxes for foreign property buyers in Spain involve a mix of purchase-related levies, with rates varying based on the property's status and the autonomous community. These initial costs significantly impact the total investment, often adding 8-15% to the purchase price.

Purchase Tax (ITP vs. VAT)

Buyers of resale properties pay Impuesto sobre Transmisiones Patrimoniales (ITP), while new-build properties incur Value Added Tax (VAT) plus Stamp Duty. ITP rates typically range from 6% to 11% depending on the region and property value, whereas new-builds are subject to a standard 10% VAT (for residential) nationwide, plus Stamp Duty.

Stamp Duty (AJD)

Impuesto de Actos Jurídicos Documentados (AJD), or Stamp Duty, applies to new-build property purchases and certain legal documents related to the transaction. This tax usually ranges from 0.5% to 1.5% of the purchase price, varying by autonomous community, and is paid in addition to VAT for new properties.

Notary and Land Registry Fees

Notary and Land Registry fees are mandatory costs for formalizing the property purchase and registering ownership. Notary fees are scaled based on the property value, typically ranging from €600 to €1,000, while Land Registry fees are slightly less, usually between €400 and €800.

Legal Fees and Due Diligence

Engaging a local lawyer for legal due diligence is highly recommended and constitutes a significant part of initial transaction costs. Legal fees typically range from 0.5% to 1.5% of the purchase price, plus VAT, ensuring the property is free of encumbrances and the transaction adheres to Spanish law. How Does Property Type Influence Spanish Tax Liabilities?

The type of property purchased, specifically whether it's a new-build or resale and its intended use (residential vs. commercial), directly impacts the applicable purchase taxes. These distinctions are critical for accurately calculating initial investment costs.

New-Build vs. Resale Properties

New-build properties are subject to 10% VAT plus Stamp Duty (AJD), while resale properties incur ITP, typically ranging from 6% to 11%. This fundamental difference means that while VAT is a fixed national rate for residential new-builds, ITP offers regional variation, which can sometimes lead to lower overall purchase tax in certain autonomous communities.

Residential vs. Commercial Property

Commercial properties in Spain are generally subject to higher VAT rates (21%) for new constructions compared to residential properties. For resale commercial properties, ITP rates can also differ from residential rates, and specific regional regulations might apply, influencing the overall tax burden for investors.

What Ongoing Annual Taxes Will Foreign Owners Face?

Foreign property owners in Spain will incur several annual taxes, regardless of residency status, including local property tax, non-resident income tax, and potentially wealth tax. These recurrent expenses must be factored into the long-term cost of ownership.

Annual Property Tax (IBI)

Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles (IBI) is an annual municipal property tax calculated based on the cadastral value of the property. Rates vary by municipality but typically range from 0.4% to 1.1% of the cadastral value for urban properties and 0.3% to 0.9% for rural properties.

Non-Resident Income Tax (IRNR)

Non-resident property owners in Spain are subject to Impuesto sobre la Renta de No Residentes (IRNR) on both deemed rental income (for personal use properties) and actual rental income. For properties not rented out, a deemed income of 1.1% or 2% of the cadastral value is taxed at a flat rate (e.g., 19% for EU/EEA citizens, 24% for others in 2026), while actual rental income is taxed after certain deductions.

Wealth Tax (Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio)

Spain’s Wealth Tax applies to the net value of assets owned, including real estate, and has both national and regional variations. While a national exemption often applies (e.g., up to €700,000 per person), several autonomous communities have their own thresholds and progressive rates, and some, like Madrid, offer 100% relief. Certain regions may also levy a temporary "Solidarity Tax on Large Fortunes" on high net worth individuals.

Are There Additional Costs Beyond Direct Taxes?

Yes, foreign buyers should account for various additional costs beyond direct taxes, which contribute to the overall expense of owning and maintaining property in Spain. These include utility hookups, community fees, and essential insurance.

Utility Connection and Service Charges

Connecting utilities like electricity, water, and gas to a newly purchased property incurs one-off connection fees and ongoing service charges. These initial setup costs can range from €200 to €1,000 depending on the property and utility provider, with monthly charges varying based on consumption.

Community Fees (if applicable)

Properties within urbanizations or apartment buildings are typically subject to mandatory community fees, covering shared services and maintenance. These fees, decided by the community of owners, can range from €50 to several hundred euros per month, depending on the amenities (pools, gardens, security) and size of the community.

Insurance Costs

Property insurance is essential in Spain, covering risks such as fire, flood, and theft, and is often mandatory if a mortgage is involved. Annual premiums for comprehensive home insurance typically range from €200 to €600, depending on the property's value, location, and coverage selected.

How Does Selling Property in Spain Affect Foreigners' Taxes?

Selling property in Spain as a foreigner triggers several taxes, primarily Capital Gains Tax and a local municipal gain tax, plus a mandatory retention for non-residents. These selling costs can significantly impact the net proceeds from a sale.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

Foreign sellers are subject to Spanish Capital Gains Tax (CGT) on the profit made from the property sale. The current CGT rate for non-residents from EU/EEA countries is 19%, while for those from other countries, it is 24%. This is calculated on the difference between the purchase price (plus acquisition costs) and the sale price (minus selling costs). Read more about CGT in our European Capital Gains Tax Guide.

Plusvalía Municipal (Local Capital Gains Tax)

The Plusvalía Municipal, or Impuesto sobre el Incremento de Valor de los Terrenos de Naturaleza Urbana, is a municipal tax levied on the increase in the value of the land portion of the property. This tax is calculated based on the cadastral value of the land and the number of years of ownership, with rates and methodologies varying by municipality. It is usually paid by the seller, though sometimes negotiated.

Seller's Retention (3% Non-Resident Seller)

When a non-resident sells a property in Spain, the buyer is legally obligated to withhold 3% of the sale price and pay it directly to the Spanish tax authorities. This 3% retention acts as an advance payment towards the seller's potential Capital Gains Tax liability. The seller then claims any excess or pays any shortfall upon filing their CGT declaration.

What Tax Incentives or Reliefs Are Available for Overseas Investors?

Spain offers certain indirect incentives and international agreements that can benefit overseas property investors, mitigating some tax burdens. Understanding these can lead to more favorable investment outcomes.

Golden Visa Program Benefits

While not a direct tax incentive, Spain's Golden Visa program, requiring a minimum €500,000 property investment, offers residency benefits that can indirectly affect tax planning. Gaining residency can alter tax obligations and allow access to resident-specific tax regimes, though full tax residency implies taxation on worldwide income. Learn more in our Spain Golden Visa Guide.

Tax Agreements to Prevent Double Taxation

Spain has signed numerous Double Taxation Treaties (DTTs) with countries worldwide to prevent individuals from being taxed twice on the same income or assets. These agreements are crucial for foreign investors as they clarify which country has the right to tax specific types of income, potentially reducing the overall tax burden for non-residents.

What are the Regional Variations in Spanish Property Taxes?

Spain's decentralized tax system means that rates for certain property taxes, particularly ITP and Wealth Tax, vary significantly across its 17 autonomous communities. These regional differences are critical considerations for property location choices.

Autonomous Community Differences in ITP and Wealth Tax

The Impuesto sobre Transmisiones Patrimoniales (ITP) rates for resale properties, as well as Wealth Tax thresholds and rates, are largely determined by each autonomous community. For example, ITP can range from 6% in some regions like Madrid up to 11% in others like Valencia or Catalonia for higher value properties, creating substantial cost differences for buyers.

Specific Regional Incentives or Surcharges

Some autonomous communities offer specific tax incentives, such as reduced ITP rates for young buyers or properties in rural areas, while others may impose surcharges or additional taxes. It's essential to research the exact tax regulations of the specific region where you plan to invest, as these can significantly alter the overall cost breakdown.

How Can Foreign Buyers Optimize Their Tax Position in Spain?

Optimizing your tax position in Spain as a foreign buyer involves strategic planning, professional advice, and a clear understanding of your residency status. Proactive measures can lead to substantial savings over time.

Seek Professional Tax Advice

Engaging a qualified Spanish tax advisor specializing in non-resident property ownership is the most effective way to optimize your tax position. A professional can provide tailored advice on deductions, legal structures, and regional tax variations applicable to your specific situation, ensuring compliance and efficiency.

Understand Residency Implications

Your residency status significantly impacts your tax obligations in Spain; non-residents are taxed differently from tax residents. Understanding the 183-day rule and other criteria for tax residency is crucial, as becoming a tax resident means being taxed on your worldwide income, not just Spanish assets.

Structuring Your Purchase

The way a property is purchased – whether as an individual, through a company, or jointly – can have significant tax implications. Discussing different ownership structures with a legal and tax expert can help identify the most tax-efficient method for your investment goals, particularly regarding inheritance or future sale. For comparison, consider the different tax implications of investing in other regions like Dubai Property or Bali Real Estate.

Here is a summary of typical property acquisition costs for a foreign buyer in Spain:

  1. Initial Research & Due Diligence: Before making an offer, engage a lawyer for pre-purchase checks (e.g., land registry, debt). Budget 0.5-1.5% of purchase price.
  2. Reservation Deposit: Typically 1-10% of the purchase price, paid to secure the property. Non-refundable if buyer defaults, often refundable if seller defaults or due diligence reveals issues.
  3. Purchase Tax (ITP or VAT + AJD):
    • Resale: ITP (6-11% depending on region/value).
    • New-Build: 10% VAT + AJD (0.5-1.5% depending on region).
  4. Notary Fees: Costs associated with signing the public deed of sale (escritura). Scaled fee, usually €600 - €1,000.
  5. Land Registry Fees: For registering your ownership. Scaled fee, usually €400 - €800.
  6. Legal Fees: For conveying the sale, reviewing contracts, and managing the process. Typically 0.5-1.5% + VAT of the purchase price.
  7. Mortgage Costs (if applicable): Valuation fee (€300-€500), opening commission (some banks), and potentially additional notary/registry fees related to the mortgage deed.
  8. Utility Connections: If a new build or first-time connection, anticipate fees for electricity, water, gas hook-up (€200-€1,000).
